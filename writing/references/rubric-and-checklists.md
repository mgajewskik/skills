# Rubric and Checklists

## Pass/Fail rewrite checklist

Pass only if all are true:

- The main point appears in the first 1-2 sentences.
- The reader can tell who does what, by when, why it matters, and how to verify success when relevant.
- The structure is easy to scan.
- A reader could paraphrase the main message and next action after one read.
- Most sentences are 20 words or fewer, or longer ones are clearly justified.
- Active voice predominates unless passive is intentional.
- Undefined acronyms, vague pronouns, and `and/or` are removed or made explicit.
- Filler, jargon, and nominalizations are removed unless needed for precision.
- The user's meaning and voice are preserved.
- The revision is clearer, not merely shorter.
- Certainty matches evidence.

## Pass/Fail feedback checklist

Pass only if all are true:

- Feedback points to concrete text patterns or excerpts.
- Feedback starts with the highest-leverage issue.
- Feedback includes at least one strength to keep.
- Feedback includes one repeatable practice habit.
- Feedback gives the user a quick self-check they can run on the next draft.
- Feedback is concise enough to use immediately.

## 8-criteria scoring rubric

Score each category from 1 to 5.
Critical categories: clarity, reader-task fit, structure, voice preservation, and factual fidelity.
Target: no critical category below 4.
Target for strong outputs: all categories at 4 or better.

1. Clarity
- 1: hard to understand without rereading
- 3: mostly understandable but muddy in places
- 5: meaning is immediately clear

2. Concision
- 1: padded and repetitive
- 3: some waste remains
- 5: tight without losing substance

3. Structure
- 1: ideas appear in the wrong order
- 3: workable but uneven
- 5: main point first, clean progression

4. Reader-task fit
- 1: writer-centered; reader goal unclear
- 3: reader can infer the goal
- 5: genre, order, and detail match the reader's job

5. Actionability
- 1: no clear takeaway or action
- 3: action exists but is incomplete
- 5: action, owner, timing, and outcome are clear

6. Tone fit
- 1: tone clashes with audience or medium
- 3: acceptable but off in places
- 5: tone fits reader, context, and stakes

7. Voice preservation
- 1: sounds nothing like the user
- 3: some voice remains
- 5: still sounds like the user, only clearer

8. Factual fidelity and evidence honesty
- 1: facts or stance drifted
- 3: mostly faithful with some risk
- 5: fully faithful to source meaning, evidence level, and constraints

9. Learnability
- 1: user cannot tell what to repeat next time
- 3: some useful advice
- 5: changes and lessons are reusable

Optional: score only the most relevant 8 criteria when brevity matters; never omit factual fidelity for rewrites.

## Before publishing any artifact

- First sentence states the point, ask, status, decision, or hook.
- Reader can name the next action or takeaway after one read.
- Acronyms and necessary technical terms are defined for the target audience.
- Facts, hypotheses, recommendations, and decisions are not blurred.
- No unsupported inflated claims or AI-slop adjectives.
- Active voice by default; passive is intentional.
- Sentence average is roughly 15-20 words; sentences over 25 words are justified.
- One topic per paragraph; one main idea per sentence.
- Owner, deadline, and done-when are explicit for action artifacts.
- Format conventions match the artifact: BLUF, Diátaxis, PR template, commit convention, ADR, incident update, etc.

## Restructuring triggers

Restructure before line-editing if any are true:

- Lede appears after sentence 2 for action-oriented writing.
- First sentences of paragraphs do not form a summary.
- Same artifact mixes Diátaxis modes.
- More than one ask appears in one message.
- Exceptions come before the rule and hide the normal path.
- Reader scrolls past 200 words before seeing why the piece matters.
- A design doc lists components but no constraints, alternatives, or consequences.
- A postmortem narrates recovery but does not prevent recurrence.

## AI-slop search list

Search for these and replace, qualify, or justify:

`leverage`, `robust`, `powerful`, `comprehensive`, `seamless`, `pivotal`, `crucial`, `delve`, `foster`, `realm`, `tapestry`, `landscape`, `multifaceted`, `journey`, `unlock`, `harness`, `embark`, `intuitive`, `cutting-edge`, `world-class`, `mission-critical`, `paradigm`, `transformative`, `game-changer`, `at the intersection`, `in today's`, `I'm thrilled`, `I wanted to reach out`, `it is important to note`, `let me know if you have any questions`.

If found, ask: does this word add a specific claim, or only register?

## Anti-criteria

Fail if any are true:
- The rewrite adds facts the user did not provide.
- The rewrite swaps so much wording that the user's voice disappears.
- The rewrite chases readability scores and becomes choppy or incoherent.
- The rewrite leaves the reader guessing about the actor, action, or decision.
- The rewrite turns uncertainty into certainty.
- The rewrite removes evidence, caveats, or technical precision to sound smoother.
- The feedback is generic enough to apply to any text.
