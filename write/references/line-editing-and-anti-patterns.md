# Line Editing and Anti-Patterns

Use this reference for English-primary line editing. It does not claim equal editing coverage for every language. Preserve local idiom, register, rhythm, and voice; ask or stay conservative when language-specific judgment is material.

## Edit for clarity, not sameness

Prefer an explicit actor, action, and target when responsibility matters. Prefer active voice when it makes the actor clear; retain passive voice when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, sensitive, or the affected thing is the point. Prefer BLUF and literal wording for action-oriented, broad, global, or high-stakes readers; do not force either onto narrative, reflective, or deliberately indirect prose.

Prefer concrete nouns and direct verbs when they preserve precision. Keep terms of art, intentional repetition, qualified claims, and needed connective tissue. A long sentence is justified when splitting it would break logic, rhythm, legal meaning, or a carefully bounded condition. A flagged word is not an error by itself.

Treat sentence-length and readability metrics only as diagnostics. Investigate a dense or long sentence; do not chase a score or make prose choppy to meet a target.

## Editing pass

1. Remove throat-clearing, duplicated meaning, and empty intensifiers.
2. Replace vague pronouns and abstract claims when the reader cannot recover the referent or evidence.
3. Repair hidden actors, weak verb-plus-noun constructions, tangled modifiers, and nonparallel lists.
4. Keep subject and verb reasonably close; split stacked clauses only when comprehension improves.
5. Keep old-to-new flow and transitions where they help the reader follow an argument.
6. Read for cadence and restore the user's natural phrasing where a mechanical correction flattened it.

Useful candidates, never automatic substitutions:

- `in order to` -> `to`; `due to the fact that` -> `because`.
- `make a decision` -> `decide`; `provide an explanation` -> `explain`.
- vague praise such as `robust` -> the concrete failure mode it withstands, when one is known.
- `and/or` -> the intended alternatives, if they are known.

Retain a literal flagged word when it has a precise technical, legal, financial, or personal meaning. Retain a justified long sentence, an intentional passive construction, or a local idiom when it serves the reader better than the generic alternative.

## Anti-AI cleanup

Remove or qualify generic promotion, inflated certainty, decorative abstraction, fake enthusiasm, and corporate fog only when they add no specific meaning. Do not use a banned-word list. Check each phrase in context:

1. Does it make a concrete, testable claim?
2. Is it a needed technical or domain term?
3. Does it carry intentional voice, rhythm, or local idiom?
4. Can a specific fact, condition, or example replace it?

Examples of patterns worth checking: empty openings, topic lists with no conclusion, generic closings, unsupported superlatives, noun stacks, and repeated transition formulas. Preserve a phrase that survives the context check.

Before returning line-edited text, confirm that the revision is clearer rather than merely different. Apply the preservation, certainty, and terminology checks in `rewrite-and-safety.md`.
