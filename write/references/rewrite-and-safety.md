# Rewrite and Safety

## Diagnose before editing

Identify the primary reader, what they know, and what they must understand, decide, or do. Identify the channel, genre, stakes, length limit, and hard constraints. If a missing detail would materially change the result, ask; otherwise make the smallest safe assumption.

Use the reader's task to choose the shape. Lead with the ask, decision, status, or takeaway when the reader must act, decide, approve, review, or triage. Use a narrative only when delayed revelation serves the reader. Fix purpose, order, headings, and paragraph jobs before line editing.

Material stakes include public, legal, compliance, HR, security, medical, financial, or irreversible/high-impact operational text where misunderstanding could affect rights, safety, money, access, reputation, or live-system state. For such writing, recommend a reader test: give the draft to a representative reader and ask them to state the main point, current certainty, required action, owner, and deadline without rereading. Never claim this test was performed when it was not. Do not add the recommendation to routine internal prose.

## Preserve before polishing

Change expression, not truth. Do not change without explicit permission:

- facts, names, numbers, measurements, dates, deadlines, times, time zones, quotations, or required terms;
- the user's stance, scope, commitments, caveats, or confidence level;
- wording marked fixed, including legal, policy, compliance, or contractual language; or
- security-sensitive boundaries, including secrets, customer data, exploit detail, and internal-only identifiers.

Preserve terminology consistently: use one label for one proven referent. Keep deliberate distinctions even when terms look similar; do not merge terms merely to reduce repetition. Define a necessary technical term for a new audience rather than replacing it with a less precise one.

Keep useful signature phrasing and tone. Trim, move, or replace wording only when it creates ambiguity, needless repetition, inaccessible jargon, or friction. When preservation and polish conflict, protect meaning and factual fidelity first, stance second, voice third, and elegance last.

## Match certainty to evidence

Keep observations, hypotheses, confirmed causes, decisions, recommendations, risks, and open questions distinct. Never turn a possibility into a confirmed cause or a planned action into a completed one. If the source overclaims, preserve the claim only when asked and flag the evidence risk; otherwise use a visible qualifier.

For operational text, distinguish symptom from impact, mitigation from prevention, risk from issue, owner from stakeholder, and done from planned. Do not add evidence the user did not provide. Use clearly marked placeholders only when they let the artifact remain usable.

## Rewrite sequence

1. State the reader's required outcome and preserve list.
2. Put the main point where the reader needs it.
3. Reorder sections and paragraphs for reader flow, not discovery order.
4. Make evidence, uncertainty, actors, decisions, and next actions explicit when relevant.
5. Apply line edits only after the structure works.
6. Check that the reader can paraphrase the main point and next action after one read.

Return the requested artifact without a routine rationale. Disclose only a changed certainty, unresolved factual ambiguity, fixed-language risk, or major structure or genre change. A major change is one that materially reorders the piece, replaces its form, or changes the reader's path to the point.
