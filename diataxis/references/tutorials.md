# Tutorials

Primary source: https://diataxis.fr/tutorials/

## What a tutorial is

A tutorial is a **lesson**.

Its purpose is not to help the reader finish real work. Its purpose is to provide a **successful learning experience** in which the learner acquires skill by doing.

Tutorials are for readers who are **at study**.

## The writer’s obligations

In a tutorial, responsibility lies mostly with the teacher:
- choose what the learner will do
- make the path logical
- make success achievable
- protect the learner from avoidable failure
- ensure the learner encounters the right tools, actions, and concepts

The learner’s job is mainly to follow.

## Core principles

- Show the learner where they are going.
- Deliver visible results early and often.
- Maintain a narrative of what the learner should expect.
- Point out what the learner should notice.
- Encourage repetition where possible.
- Focus on the concrete and particular.
- Ignore options and alternatives that are not required for this lesson.
- Aspire to perfect reliability.

## Anti-pedagogical temptations

These usually damage tutorials:
- explanation
- abstraction
- generalization
- choices
- extra information

The key rule is: **do not try to teach by explaining**.

Let the learner learn through the experience you create.

## Language patterns

Prefer:
- `In this tutorial, we will…`
- `First, do x. Now do y.`
- `You will notice that…`
- `The output should look something like…`
- `Let’s check…`

Avoid:
- `In this tutorial you will learn…`
- long conceptual digressions
- menus of alternatives
- dense background explanation inside the main path

Use first-person plural (`we`) naturally to reinforce guided participation.

## Tutorial checklist

Pass when most are true:
- the learner is guided through a single managed path
- every major step has an expected result or observation
- the environment is intentionally simplified or controlled
- the content builds confidence through success
- explanation is minimal and linked out when deeper background is needed
- options and branching are suppressed unless absolutely necessary

Warning signs:
- the page expects significant reader judgment
- the page branches heavily on real-world conditions
- the page keeps stopping to explain concepts in depth
- the page reads like a recipe for work rather than a lesson

## Minimal rewrite moves toward tutorial form

- replace broad goal language with a concrete lesson outcome
- remove optional branches and keep one successful route
- add expected outputs and reassurance cues
- trim or move explanation into linked explanation pages
- make physical or interface actions explicit if the learner may not know them
